Introduction

Globally, forests are recognised as valuable for their intrinsic worth, the biodiversity they support, the ecosystem services they provide, their value for timber and non-timber products and the potential that they have for ameliorating the impacts of climate change. However, we still have a limited understanding of their dynamics and capacity to adapt to change; addressing this knowledge gap requires long term monitoring approaches. The data collected from long term permanent plot stem inventories allows assessment of timber productivity, stand structure (e.g., Manokaran and LaFrankie 1990), stand diversity (e.g., Lee et al. 2002), carbon storage (e.g., Baker et al. 2004) and, over time, provides insights into the dynamics of forests (e.g., Lewis et al. 2004).

The CSIRO permanent rainforest plots are located within 60 km of the north Queensland coast between Mackay (21.5ºS, 149ºE) and the Iron Range on Cape York Peninsula (12.5ºS, 143ºE). North-eastern Australia is topographically diverse, and the plots span much of the geographical variation in environmental gradients across the study area. The climate is tropical with mean annual rainfall ranging from 1200 mm to over 8000 mm on the higher coastal ranges. Seventeen of the plots are located within the Wet Tropics Bioregion (sensu Department of Environment 2013) between 19.4ºS, 146.5ºE and 15.7ºS, 145.3ºE. While covering only 0.24% of the Australian continent, the Wet Tropics bioregion contains very high levels of diversity and endemism of flora and fauna (Metcalfe and Ford 2009; Stork et al. 2011; Bradford et al. in press). Continuous areas of rainforest account for approximately 6300 km² of the region (Tracey 1982) and represent by far the largest area of rainforest in Australia. At the northern end of the study area, the rainforests of Cape York Peninsula form a largely discontinuous band that have close affinities to the flora of New Guinea which lies just to the north. At the southern end of the study area, the rainforests of the Mackay area contain floristics elements representing both tropical and sub-tropical rainforest.

Indigenous people have occupied north Queensland rainforests for at least 8000 years (Hill et al. 2011), though traditional use of the area had largely ceased in the second half of the 20th century. Due to the low population density, it is unlikely that the original inhabitants contributed significantly to rainforest dynamics except in small areas, with only very selective timber harvesting, clearing and burning taking place. In contrast, during the 20th century, 50% of the rainforest area in the wet tropical area of North Queensland was cleared (Winter 1987) and a large proportion was selectively logged on at least one occasion. With minor exceptions on two plots, all CSIRO permanent plots were established in unlogged forest. All plots were thereafter protected from selective logging activities. In 1988 almost all of the remaining rainforest area under State Forest and National Park in the state of Queensland, including 16 CSIRO permanent plots, was protected under World Heritage listing as the Wet Tropics of Queensland World Heritage Area. The remaining four plots are protected in National Park or Forest Reserve.

The major drivers of forest dynamics in the study area are non-anthropogenic disturbances, especially cyclones, but including drought, erosion and pathogen outbreaks (see Metcalfe et al. 2008; Metcalfe and Bradford 2008; Murphy et al. 2013). Seventeen of the 20 plots have been impacted by at least one of these disturbances since establishment with cyclones causing major disturbances on 11 plots and minor disturbances on a further four (Table 1).

We present data from 20 CSIRO permanent rainforest plots, each 0.5 ha (100 m × 500 m), located in northern Queensland, Australia from establishment (1971–1980) to 2013. Data consists of Diameter at Breast Height, or 1.3 m height above ground (DBH) measurements of all stems ≥10 cm DBH taken at each census, stem height at establishment and in 1998–2001 (or at recruitment into ≥10 cm DBH size), and spatial position of each stem. The first two plots were established in 1971 and the remainder established regularly through the 1970s with the last established in 1980. Census intervals range between 2–15 years (mean = 3.0 years). In addition we present understory floristic data, an auxiliary species list, and a list of voucher specimens from all plots. At the initial census of all 20 plots, 8324 living stems were enumerated comprising 490 species, 238 genera and 75 families. At the completion of the latest census for each plot, 8353 living stems were enumerated. Due to the large variation in plot environmental conditions, no species dominate any structural strata although the families Lauraceae, Rutaceae and Myrtaceae contribute a large number of species which reflects northern Queensland rainforests in general (Metcalfe and Ford 2009). At establishment, stand density ranged from 476 to 1104 stems/ha and basal area ranged from 28.6 to 63.3 m²/ha.

The data collected from the CSIRO permanent rainforest plots has been valuable to date in enabling structural and floristic descriptions of Australian tropical forests, in supplying numerous voucher specimens for herbaria and providing focal areas for intensive ecological studies. With time, the data has given insights into forest dynamics including growth, recruitment and mortality in representative forest types. The plots are now well placed to monitor any effects of a changing climate on species and forest dynamics.

Abstract: We present repeated stem measurement data from 20 0.5-ha (100 × 50 m) permanent rain forest plots in northern Queensland, Australia from 1971 to 2013. The plots have a rainfall range of 1200 to 3500 mm, represent 11 vegetation types, six parent materials, and range from 15 to 1200 m above sea level. Except for minor disturbances associated with selective logging on two plots, the plots were established in old growth forest and all plots have thereafter been protected. Plots were regularly censused and at each census the diameter at breast height (DBH) of all stems ≥10 cm DBH were recorded. Data is presented for 10998 individual stems with plot stem densities at establishment ranging from 476 to 1104 stems/ha. Due to the wide geographical range of the plots, no species dominate, although the families Lauraceae, Rutaceae, and Myrtaceae contribute a large number of species. Basal area values at establishment ranged from 28.6 to 63.3 m²/ha and showed no trend of increasing or decreasing over time due mainly to regular disturbance and recovery from natural events such as cyclones. In addition to stems ≥10 cm DBH data, we present height data, floristic data from understory stems (≥50 cm height to <10 cm DBH), an auxiliary species list (including vines, epiphytes, ferns, grasses, herbs, and other life forms), and a list of voucher specimens lodged in herbaria. The data collected from the 20 plots provides an insight into the floristics, structure, and long-term forest dynamics of Australian tropical rain forests and allows direct comparisons to be made with long-term monitoring plots at a global scale.

A. Overall project description

Identity: CSIRO permanent rainforest plots, 1971–2013

Originator: G. C. Stocker (Commonwealth Forest and Timber Bureau, Atherton, later CSIRO).

Period of Study: July 1970–November 2013 (ongoing)

Objective: To record and analyse patterns of stem growth and stand dynamics in a broad range of permanently reserved plots in rainforest in North Queensland, Australia.

Abstract: The objective of the long term plot series was to build a comprehensive database of rainforest stem growth, extending over a useful range of sites and a biologically meaningful span of years. To provide for analysis and interpretation, the growth record needed to include descriptions of forest structure, floristic composition and stand changes in response to environmental disturbance. This required a high standard of repeated mensuration and continuity, in a variety of rainforest sites whose location is definable in terms of major environmental gradients. To investigate the floristic and structural consequences of a given type of disturbance, several strategies were developed; (a) to establish a series of 0.5 ha reference plots where the growth, recruitment and mortality of component species could be monitored for long periods. Where possible, these plots were located in generally representative unlogged forest, (b) to study the reproductive biology and early growth characteristics of the major (ecologically and numerically) species found in the permanent plot series, and (c) to use data from (a) and (b) to develop models which will predict floristic and structural change in different forest types subjected to disturbances varying in nature, frequency and degree.

Sources of funding: Commonwealth Forest and Timber Bureau (1971–1978), CSIRO internal funding (1978–1992), Tropical Rainforest Ecology and Management and the Rainforest Cooperative Research Centres (1992–2006), Earthwatch (2006). The most recent censuses have been supported by the Long-Term Ecological Research Network sub facility, part of the Australian Government's Terrestrial Ecosystems Research Network (http://www.tern.org.au/Long-Term-Ecological-Research-Network-LTERN-pg17872.html).

Background: In 1971, about 100 years after the first European commercial exploitation of rainforests in North Queensland, work commenced on the first in a series of specifically planned, long term research plots designed to examine natural processes in rainforests rather than responses to, or suitability for, timber extraction. This work was carried out by the Atherton based staff of the Forest and Timber Bureau within the Commonwealth Department of National Development led by Geoff Stocker. Some plots were opportunistically located ahead of logging operations and showed some bias towards commercially attractive species, however, these species are often common and widespread and the plots are considered representative of several widespread forest types. Several such species in the genus Flindersia were initially recorded to seedling and sapling stage on eight plots. This work was abandoned in the 1980s and the data is not presented in this data paper.

Subsequent to establishment, CSIRO staff and volunteers based at the Tropical Forest Research Centre in Atherton have maintained the project. In particular, Keith Sanderson (1971–1998) and Matt Bradford (1998–2013) have supervised each census and maintained the data.

Experimental or sampling design: The 20 CSIRO permanent plots were opportunistically located ahead of logging operations or in areas of no or minor recent disturbance (EP9 and EP38 showed evidence of slight disturbance in a section of the plot due to selective logging at least 20 years prior to establishment). Where possible, plots were located to represent the major forests and geologies of North Queensland. All plots were surveyed to enclose a projected plan view area of 0.5 ha (100 m × 50 m) with plot boundary distances corrected for slope. The resulting plot areas are presented in Table 2. Plot boundaries were surveyed with prismatic compasses, 50 m steel survey band and Abney level. The four plot corners were initially marked with treated wooden pegs and subsequently replaced with steel pickets. All plots were subdivided into 16 subplots (A to P; Fig. 1), each 25 m × 12.5 m with permanent wooden subplot markers. A 20 m buffer area around each plot was established to exclude future human disturbance. A canopy tower was located adjacent to both EP4 and EP33 from establishment until 2005 and focused on canopy sampling and photosynthetic studies. Data from the towers is not included in this data paper, however, publications resulting from data collected are included in the publications and results section. The plots were established between 1971 and 1980 and were initially censused every two years until 1991 (Table 3). Since 1991 census intervals have ranged between 2–15 years.

Research methods: At each census, the diameter of all stems ≥10 cm DBH were measured with a diameter tape (Richter 2 m, Yamayo Million 10 m) to the nearest millimetre. Measurements made by the Commonwealth Forest and Timber Bureau at plot establishment followed protocols of Queensland Department of Forestry which are similar to Condit (1988) with two major exceptions, (1) for species known to exhibit buttressing on larger specimens, the point of measurement (POM) was pre-emptively elevated above the predicted buttressing influence. Prior to 1992 this was not always done as the buttressing nature of all species was not known. Subsequently, some point of measurement marks have been raised as buttresses moved their way up stems. In addition, some POM marks were lowered or raised due to deformations on the stem. In these cases only the newly raised or lowered DBH measurement was measured and DBH changed was recorded. (2) point of measurement lines approximately 25 mm wide and perpendicular to the trunk, and an alphanumeric stem code were painted on the stem.

Rules of measurement are as follows:

1) Comments were recorded for unusual measurements that may affect the way the data is interpreted: 1 = raised or lowered point of measurement, 2 = broken top above POM, 3 = hollow stem, 4 = dead or damaged part of the stem, 5 = DBH estimated, 7 = sick stem, 8 = near dead stem, 9 = leaning >45°.

2) Stem diameters were measured in centimetres at breast height from the ground on the uphill side of the stem. Stems with DBH <10 cm were not included.

3) Stems leaning >45 degrees (comment 9) were measured 1.3 m along the stem along the underside of the stem.

4) Vines were pulled away from the stem or the diameter tape was slipped under the vines before measuring where possible. Where there was a reason why no measurement could be made, a visual estimation of the DBH was made (comment 5). This occurred when a stem had immovable vines, a strangler fig, or large and high buttresses.

5) Stems with a swelling or deformity that precludes taking a normal DBH measurement were measured above or below the deformity. If the deformity continued to <1 m above the ground then the measurement was taken above the deformity.

6) Stems with buttresses were measured at least 1 m above the highest buttress. Smaller specimens of particular species that were known to exhibit buttressing when they grow larger were measured at a higher POM to account for future buttressing. In this case the POM was decided on by the team leader.

7) Where the trunk was irregular, damaged, deformed or fluted at all heights, the POM was taken at 1.3 m. A comment of 3 or 4 was recorded for hollow or damaged stem.

8) Stems that forked above 1.3 m were measured below the fork where the stem was not swollen or deformed. Stems that forked below 1.3 m were measured as separate stems, at or close to 1.3 m.

9) Palms were included if the stem was ≥10 cm DBH below the lowest living leaf base.

10) Lianas were measured 1.3 m along the stem after they left the ground. The height of a liana was recorded as the estimated length of the stem and was generally higher than the host stem. Lianas were mapped where they were considered to originate.

 11) The diameter of each individual stem (aerial root) of a strangler fig ≥10 cm DBH was measured separately. Over time when these roots merged with each other or the host stem, then the DBH was estimated (comment 5).

Lianas ≥10 cm DBH were not included in the census at establishment, were included selectively from 1987 onwards. Consequently, the 11 stems listed in Table 4 were most likely present as stems ≥10 cm before they were recorded in the database. All other lianas in the database (8 stems) were included in the database as they were recruited into the ≥10 cm DBH size class.

The positions of all stems ≥10 cm DBH were mapped to an accuracy of ±0.5 m. The X coordinate was taken as the projected plan distance in metres along the 100 m axis starting at the subplot A–D outside edge of the plot (Fig. 1). The Y coordinate was taken as the projected plan distance in metres along the 50 m axis starting at the subplot A–M outside edge. Where stems originated within 0.5 m of each other or in a multi-stemmed case, the same coordinates were shared within the same plot.

At establishment, the height of each stem ≥10 cm DBH was estimated. Height estimations were taken as the length of the stem from ground to upmost growing tip. Estimations were regularly validated for highly visible stems using the triangulation method (base distance and slope angle) with a clinometer and measuring tape.  Height estimations were repeated in 1998 (2001 at ep37; not repeated at ep31) using the same method with the addition of a laser rangefinder (Newcon Optic 1200, Nikon Forestry 550) to aid in base distance, slope angle and direct height estimations where appropriate. Stems recruited at each census were given a height using the same method.

Stems were numbered using an alphanumeric code representing subplot (A to P) and sequential number within that subplot. This code was painted on the stem either below or above the POM line (Fig. 2). Multi-stemmed individuals branching below the POM were numbered by placing a numeral in the 'hundreds' before the stem number. For example: in the same subplot, P005, P105, and P205 are separate trunks of the same individual branching at or below 1.3 m.

 

Fig. 2. An example of the point of measurement and stem numbering system on the stem A33, Agathis robusta at EP40. Photograph by Dan Metcalfe.

 

At each census following establishment, new stems recruiting into the ≥10 cm DBH size class were measured, mapped and assigned a height. All stems ≥10 cm DBH that had died since the last census were noted and no attempt was made to assign a cause of death. Stems that were dead or snapped below the painted POM line were considered dead. The nature and extent of major disturbances (e.g., cyclones, erosion) in each plot were noted and mapped.

Initially plant species identification followed the Queensland Herbarium (see Bostock and Holland 2010). For this data paper, these names were matched with the Atlas of Living Australia http://www.ala.org.au/ standards. At establishment, a voucher specimen was collected for a representative of each species at each plot for all stems ≥10 cm DBH. These vouchers were lodged at the Australian Tropical Herbarium (CNS, formerly QRS) and various herbaria around the world. Since establishment, vouchers for any new species have been collected in a semi-formal manner. Wood density values for species represented in the data have been collated from the available literature and from field sampling. These are not presented in the data paper and can be accessed upon application to the principle investigators.

II.B.2. Survey of understory stems ≥50 cm in height to <10 cm DBH

Site description: same as II.B.1

Experimental or sampling design: Plot design and establishment the same as II.B.1.

Research methods: The floristics of all understory woody tree and shrub species was assessed for all plots at establishment. Within each of the 16 subplots, the presence or absence of each species was recorded in two size classes; (1) individuals ≥ 50 cm to 3 m in height and (2) individuals >3 m in height and <10 cm DBH. A voucher specimen was collected for a representative of each species not present as a stem ≥10 cm DBH on each plot. These vouchers were lodged at CNS and various herbaria around the world. All plots are progressively being re-assessed and vouchers for any new species are collected in a semi-formal manner. Only data collected at establishment is presented in this data paper and subsequent data can be accessed upon application to the principle investigators.

Project personnel: Principal investigators, Bernie Hyland, Tony Irvine, Keith Sanderson, Greg Unwin.

II.B.3. Survey of auxiliary species

Site description: same as II.B.1

Experimental or sampling design: Plot design and establishment the same as II.B.1.

Research method: A species list of plants not recorded as stems ≥10 cm DBH or as an understory woody tree or shrub was made for each plot at establishment. Life forms included climber, club moss, cycad, epiphyte, fern, grass, herb, lily, palm, pandan, sedge and vine. Voucher specimens were collected where feasible. These vouchers were lodged at CNS and various herbaria around the world.

Project personnel: Principal investigators, Keith Sanderson, Tony Irvine, Greg Unwin.

Definition of variable codes: Descriptions of variable codes are given for those variables needing additional explanation beyond that provided in the data table above.

epNumber. CSIRO experimental plot number ranging from ep2 to ep44.

stemNumber. An alphanumeric number that is unique to a stem within a plot.

family. The taxonomic family name following the Atlas of Living Australia http://www.ala.org.au/. In records where this value is null, this indicates that the taxon of the stem has not been determined.

genus. The taxonomic genus name following the Atlas of Living Australia http://www.ala.org.au/. In records where this value is null, this indicates that the taxon of the stem has not been determined.

taxon. The species' latin name following Atlas of Living Australia http://www.ala.org.au/. In records where this value is null, this indicates that the taxon of the stem has not been determined. Where no match was found with the Atlas of Living Australia database, the name in taxonAuth either (1) remained the same as described by Queensland Herbarium (see Bostock and Holland 2010) or (2) remained a local assigned name.

taxonAuth. The species' latin name with author following Atlas of Living Australia http://www.ala.org.au/.

 ALA_APNI_Taxon_LSID. The "Life Science Identifier" for the species from the Australian Plant Name Index (IBIS database, Centre for Australian National Biodiversity Research, Australian Government, Canberra http://www.anbg.gov.au/apni/, sourced from the Atlas of Living Australia http://www.ala.org.au. In records where this value is null, this indicates that either; no match was found for the value in "taxonAuth" with a name from the "apni.taxon" collection in the Atlas of Living Australia; or the taxon of the stem has not been determined. The APNI data used was obtained through the Atlas of Living Australia National Species List Linked Data Services available at http://biodiversity.org.au, and is used under a Creative Commons Attribution 3.0 license http://creativecommons.org/licenses/by/3.0/.

ALA_APNI_Taxon_URI. A URI for the "Taxon Concept" referred to in the ALA_APNI_Taxon_LSID column.

year. See Table 3 for actual stem census dates for each plot.

dbh_centimetres. The DBH (1.3 m) of stems ≥10 cm DBH in the plot confines. See research methods for a full explanation.

comment. Comments are recorded for unusual measurements that may affect the way the data is interpreted. See research methods for a full explanation.

establishmentHeight_metres. The height estimated for each stem ≥10 cm DBH when the particular plot was first established. See Table 3 for establishment dates. Stem heights estimated at later dates or when a stem is recruited into the ≥10 cm DBH size are recorded in the variable code height_metres.

height_metres. The height of any stem recruited since establishment, or the height estimated in the second major height census in 1998 (2001 for ep37). In records where this value is null, this indicates that the stem is not new at the year specified in variable 'year' or was not a stem ≥10 cm DBH in 1998 (2001 for ep37).

status. Dead or alive for the year given in the variable 'year'. A record showing dead will have a null value in the dbh_centimetres column.

C. Data Anomalies

A small number of stems have a DBH <10 cm. In these cases the stem has legitimately shrunk below 10 cm DBH or the stem was damaged in some way resulting in a measurement <10 cm DBH. These stems will continue to be measured.

Repeated stem height estimates are not presented for EP31. Estimates were not made during the 2005 census, and in 2006 a severe cyclone passed over the plot causing considerable structural damage resulting in misleading height estimates.

History of data set usage: See publications and results above. Throughout the history of the plots the value of the data has been questioned and reviewed due to logistic and funding issues. Originally each plot was censused every two years until 1991. With the cessation of the logging industry in North Queensland in 1988, a number of access tracks became impassable by vehicle making it harder to reach some plots. Partly for this reason and partly due to the changing funding structure of CSIRO, plots were censused at a longer interval from 1991. With time, the value of the long term data has been recognized and in 2011 the re-census of the plots was funded by the Terrestrial Long-Term Ecological Research Network sub facility, part of the Australian Government's Terrestrial Ecosystems Research Network http://www.tern.org.au/.

The stem diameter measurement data set is updated at an approximately 4-year interval for each plot. The understory data is re-surveyed opportunistically for each plot and the current data can be obtained by contacting the principle investigators. Voucher specimens are taken opportunistically. This project is planned to continue indefinitely.
